Most ecommerce operations problems don't come from a single failure. They come from tasks that were meant to happen regularly, didn't, and then piled up.

Response times slip because no one was checking the helpdesk daily. Inventory runs out because the audit didn't happen this month. Product listings go live with errors because the QA step got skipped when things were busy.

This checklist is a starting point. The exact tasks will vary based on your platform, your volume, and your operation. But the structure — daily, weekly, monthly — is a useful framework for any UK ecommerce business.

## Daily operations tasks

These need to happen every working day without exception. If they're not happening, problems are accumulating.

**Customer support**
- Check all support inboxes (email, live chat, helpdesk tickets)
- Respond to open tickets within your SLA target
- Escalate anything outside standard handling rules
- Flag any unusual patterns (spike in WISMO queries, surge in complaints about a specific product)

**Orders**
- Process any manual orders or those requiring intervention
- Check for failed payments and follow up
- Confirm any orders flagged by fraud detection
- Review any order modifications or cancellations requested

**Returns and refunds**
- Process returns received at warehouse
- Issue refunds for approved requests
- Update RMA statuses
- Flag any returns with unusual reasons or high value

**Monitoring**
- Check for any platform alerts or errors
- Review automated tool notifications (inventory alerts, payment failures)
- Scan for order or listing anomalies

## Weekly operations tasks

These don't need daily attention but can't be left for months. Weekly cadence keeps them manageable.

**Inventory review**
- Check stock levels against your minimum thresholds
- Identify any SKUs approaching reorder point
- Raise restocking requests to suppliers as needed
- Investigate any inventory discrepancies between platform and warehouse

**Catalogue audit**
- Review new products added for accuracy (titles, descriptions, images, pricing)
- Check for out-of-stock products that need updating
- Review any products that have changed from the supplier
- QA a sample of listings against your standards

**Reporting**
- Pull key metrics: order volume, refund rate, support ticket volume, response times
- Note any unusual patterns compared to the previous week
- Flag anything requiring attention

**Process review**
- Note any recurring issues that suggest a process needs updating
- Update SOPs for any process that changed this week
- Flag any tool or integration issues that emerged

## Monthly operations tasks

Monthly tasks tend to be the first dropped when things are busy. They're also the ones that prevent bigger problems later.

**Listing QA**
- Full audit of a sample of product listings
- Check SEO titles and descriptions against current standards
- Review image quality across key categories
- Update pricing for any products with supplier price changes

**SOP review**
- Review standard operating procedures for accuracy
- Update any SOPs that have drifted from actual practice
- Document any new processes that were handled ad hoc this month

**Supplier and 3PL review**
- Review supplier performance: delivery times, quality issues, communication
- Check 3PL performance: error rates, processing times, claims
- Address any outstanding issues from the previous month

**Returns analysis**
- Review return rates by product category
- Identify products with unusually high return rates
- Investigate top return reasons and flag anything that suggests product or listing issues

**Financial reconciliation**
- Reconcile refunds against accounting records
- Review chargeback status and outcomes
- Flag any discrepancies for finance review

## What to automate vs what to delegate

Some operational tasks are better automated. Some need human attention. Knowing the difference saves time and money.

**Automate:**
- Low-stock alerts (set thresholds in your inventory tool)
- Order confirmation and shipping notification emails
- Review requests post-delivery
- Basic FAQ responses (AI chatbot handles these 24/7)
- Scheduled report generation

**Delegate to a dedicated operator:**
- Customer support responses that require judgement
- Order exceptions and edge cases
- Catalogue management that requires quality control
- Returns that need investigation
- Anything that touches your brand voice directly

**Keep for yourself:**
- Supplier relationship decisions
- Pricing strategy changes
- Major product and range decisions
- Anything that changes your brand positioning

## When it's time to get help

The checklist above represents roughly 25 to 40 hours of operational work per week for a mid-volume ecommerce store (500–2,000 orders monthly). That's a full-time role.

If you're doing this yourself, you'll recognise the signs:

- Tasks from this list are regularly skipped or delayed
- Response times to customers have crept up
- You're thinking about operations tasks when you should be thinking about growth
- You've had at least one avoidable problem in the last month — an oversell, a slow return, a batch of poorly uploaded products

That's when a dedicated operator makes sense. Not a freelancer who takes tasks off a list — a dedicated person who owns your operations, follows your SOPs, and produces weekly evidence that the work is being done properly.
