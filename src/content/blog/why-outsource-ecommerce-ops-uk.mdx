Running a UK ecommerce business in 2026 is not the same as it was five years ago. The costs are higher, the expectations are greater, and the operational complexity has increased at every level. Most founders are still trying to handle it themselves.

That's starting to change.

## The hiring squeeze

The 2024 Autumn Budget made hiring more expensive. Employer National Insurance rose to 15%, and the threshold dropped to £5,000. A junior operations hire at £28,000 now costs closer to £34,000 in total employer cost — before recruitment fees, onboarding time, and management overhead.

That's before you factor in the difficulty of finding someone who actually knows ecommerce operations. Not someone who's "used Shopify before." Someone who can manage a helpdesk, coordinate with a 3PL, handle chargebacks, upload products correctly, and document processes without being told to.

Those people exist. They're expensive, they take time to find, and they don't stay forever.

## The operations bottleneck

Most ecommerce businesses hit a wall somewhere between £100k and £500k revenue. Not a growth wall — an operations wall.

The founder is handling customer emails at 11pm. Orders are being processed manually. Returns are taking two weeks to resolve. Products aren't being uploaded because there's no time. The helpdesk is a Gmail inbox with 400 unread messages.

This is not a rare situation. This is what scaling an ecommerce business actually looks like for most UK founders.

The business isn't broken. The operations layer isn't resourced properly. Every hour spent processing refunds is an hour not spent on growth, product, or marketing. The opportunity cost is significant — and it compounds.

## What managed operations actually means

Managed ecommerce operations isn't a new concept, but the model has become more practical and more affordable.

The idea is simple: instead of hiring a full-time employee or cobbling together a mix of freelancers, you work with a service that provides a dedicated, trained operator for a flat monthly fee.

Your operator is assigned to your store. They learn your processes, your tools, your brand voice. They handle customer support, orders, returns, catalogue management, and inventory — whatever's within the agreed scope. They work your hours, follow your SOPs, and produce a weekly report of everything they've done.

The difference from a freelancer is accountability. The difference from an agency is dedication. Your operator works on your store, not on twelve others.

## Who is it for?

Managed operations isn't the right fit for every business. Here's who it works well for:

**Founders doing operational tasks themselves.** If you're processing orders, answering support tickets, or uploading products personally, that's a clear sign your time is being misallocated.

**Businesses that have outgrown their current setup.** You've tried a part-time VA here, a freelancer there. The results were inconsistent. You need something more structured.

**Businesses not yet at full hiring scale.** You can't justify a full-time operations hire yet, but you need more than occasional freelance support. The gap between "I can't afford to hire" and "I can't keep doing this alone" is exactly where this model sits.

**Businesses that value accountability.** You want audit trails. You want weekly reports. You want to know what's been done without needing to ask every day.

## The numbers

At £999/month, a managed operator costs £11,988 per year on monthly billing, or £9,990 on an annual plan.

Compare that to a UK junior hire. Salary at £28,000, employer NI at approximately £3,000, pension contribution at £1,000, recruitment fees at £2,000 to £5,000. You're looking at £34,000 to £37,000 in year one, for someone who may not have specific ecommerce operations experience and who will need significant time to train.

The cost difference is not marginal. It's roughly three times more expensive to hire in-house.

## What changes

The change isn't just financial. The operational improvement is often more significant.

When a dedicated operator takes over customer support, response times drop. When orders are processed by someone focused exclusively on that task, errors decrease. When catalogue management is handled daily rather than squeezed in between other work, the quality improves.

More importantly, the founder gets their time back. Not partially — properly. When you stop handling tier-1 support and order processing, you reclaim 10 to 20 hours a week. That time goes into the parts of the business that actually require you.

## The decision

Outsourcing operations is not a step down. It's a business decision — one that an increasing number of UK ecommerce founders are making because the alternative is unsustainable.

The question isn't whether you can afford a dedicated operator. The question is whether you can afford to keep doing it yourself.

If you're spending hours a week on operational tasks that could be handled by someone else, the answer is clear.
